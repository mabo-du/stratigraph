# **Structural Foundations and Computational Architectures for Modern Harris Matrix Software**

## **1\. The Epistemic and Ontological Conventions of the Harris Matrix**

The conceptualization of the Harris Matrix by Edward C. Harris in 1973 precipitated a fundamental paradigm shift in archaeological methodology, transitioning the discipline from the excavation of arbitrary horizontal spits to the rigorous, stratigraphic documentation of single contexts.1 Prior to this innovation, the chronological sequence of an archaeological site was inextricably and often confusingly bound to its physical, three-dimensional representation in section drawings. The Harris Matrix abstracted this relationship, creating a purely topological, four-dimensional diagram of relative time that decoupled the chronological sequence from the physical coordinates of the deposits.1 Developing a computational generator for these matrices requires an exhaustive understanding of the underlying conventions, graphical standards, and topological rules that govern archaeological stratigraphy.  
The topological logic of the Harris Matrix rests on a strictly limited set of allowable relationships between units of archaeological stratification, commonly referred to as contexts or stratigraphic units (SUs).3 Harris originally defined three primary connections that dictate the vertical flow of time within the diagram. The first is a state of no direct stratigraphic connection, wherein units lack any physical or chronological relationship that can be observed or inferred from the stratigraphy.3 The second is the relationship of superposition, denoting that units lie in a direct, observable sequence of deposition.3 In this state, one unit is physically, and therefore chronologically, later than the other, forming the fundamental "above" and "below" edges of the directed graph. The third primary connection is correlation, indicating that two or more units are physically equal.3 This correlation applies to units that were parts of a once-whole deposit or feature interface that has subsequently been severed by human activity or natural erosion, or it applies to identical contexts that were excavated and assigned different numbers in separate operations.4  
As the methodology matured and transitioned into computational systems, the formalization of relationships expanded to encompass nuanced chronological states, most notably the distinction between "equal to" and "contemporary with." While Edward Harris's earliest formulations did not strictly differentiate between equal and contemporary relationships, modern stratigraphic theory and computational architecture necessitate treating them as distinct epistemic categories.6 The "equal" relationship asserts an ontological sameness; the nodes represent the exact same physical entity. Conversely, "contemporary with" asserts that two physically distinct entities share the same temporal horizon based on non-stratigraphic dating evidence.4 This relationship is typically determined by material culture associations or architectural analysis, such as two distinct walls that are "bonded to" each other, indicating they were constructed in a single event, or a series of separate postholes that form the outline of a single timber building.4 Modern matrix software, such as Stratify, incorporates specific algorithms to validate "contemporary with" relationships by checking absolute date entries (such as center-year estimates) against user-defined temporal thresholds to flag illogical chronological assertions.6  
The graphical representation of these relationships follows strict conventions designed to convey structural and temporal meaning instantaneously. In mathematical terms, the Harris Matrix is structured as a directed acyclic graph (DAG) where the flow of time is strictly unidirectional along the vertical axis, progressing from the most recent deposits at the top to the oldest at the bottom.7 Early iterations of the matrix, often drawn by hand, utilized plain rectangular boxes for all units regardless of their physical nature. However, modern formal diagramming standards, successfully pioneered and formalized by applications like the Harris Matrix Composer (HMC), mandate the explicit graphical separation of the two basic types of stratification units.7 Material deposits, which are positive units representing accumulations of soil, debris, or masonry, are strictly represented by rectangular or square nodes.7 Immaterial features, which are negative units representing interfaces of destruction such as cuts, pits, or robber trenches, are represented by circular or oval nodes.7 This visual dichotomy is crucial for rapid interpretive analysis during post-excavation. Every node must possess a unique identifier, typically an integer or alphanumeric string, and the layout must visually respect the Law of Superposition.7  
A critical computational convention in the processing of Harris Matrices is the absolute elimination of redundant relationships through transitive reduction. Because the matrix relies on an unbroken chain of transitive logic, if Unit A is stratigraphically above Unit B, and Unit B is above Unit C, the direct assertion that Unit A is above Unit C is a stratigraphic redundancy. Field archaeologists often record these redundant relationships during primary documentation out of an abundance of caution or due to the sheer complexity of the trench. A robust Harris Matrix generator must execute a transitive reduction algorithm to parse the raw field data, identify these redundancies, and strip them from the graphical output, ensuring that the final graph displays only the essential, un-duplicated stratigraphic sequence required to establish the site's relative chronology.11

## **2\. Technical Retrospective of Existing Stratigraphic Software**

The evolution of software dedicated to generating Harris Matrices spans over three decades, revealing a technological landscape categorized by specialized legacy tools, proprietary modern suites, and a burgeoning open-source ecosystem. A meticulous technical analysis of these tools exposes the critical limitations, architectural flaws, and feature gaps that a modern open-source generator must address to succeed.  
The Bonn Archaeological Software Package (BASP) was an early pioneer in computational matrix generation. Operating initially in MS-DOS and later ported to early Windows environments, it provided a suite of tools that allowed archaeologists to manage stratigraphy alongside seriation, clustering, and spatial mapping functions.13 However, the BASP matrix tool struggled heavily with complex graphical layouts. It relied on rudimentary algorithms that frequently failed to resolve complex combinations of "H-structures"—intricate webs of cross-cutting stratigraphic relationships—resulting in ambiguous line crossings that confused the interpreter.11 Furthermore, its reliance on legacy data structures made integration with emerging relational databases highly problematic.  
To overcome the graphical and algorithmic limitations of BASP, the Max Planck Institute developed ArchEd in the late 1990s and early 2000s.2 Designed specifically for Windows 95, Windows 98, and Windows NT environments, ArchEd narrowed its focus strictly to the generation of Harris matrix diagrams, avoiding the broader ambitions of an all-encompassing archaeological database.15 ArchEd saved data in a proprietary internal format but supported the import and export of ASCII text files, specifically utilizing the extended .LST (List) format inherited from the Bonn program, alongside graphical exports to Windows Metafiles (WMF) and bitmaps.15 Despite its improved layout capabilities, ArchEd suffered from severe technical limitations. It lacked an interactive graphical canvas; users could not draw relationships by clicking and dragging between nodes. Instead, all stratigraphic links had to be entered laboriously through rigid dialogue boxes and nested menus.15 More critically, performance profiling revealed fatal memory management flaws. On contemporary systems equipped with 64MB of RAM, the software experienced severe display corruption and hard crashes when attempting to process and render datasets approaching 1,000 contexts.15 The source code was never released, and official development ceased entirely following the dissolution of the research team in 2003, rendering the software a stagnant artifact of early digital archaeology.15  
As the discipline moved toward comprehensive data management, commercial systems emerged, bringing robust graphical user interfaces but introducing severe issues regarding data silos and vendor lock-in. StratiGraf, released by Proleg, was a commercial Windows application that attempted to integrate the entire excavation database hierarchy—from site and trench down to stratigraphic units and individual artifacts—into the matrix environment.15 However, StratiGraf utilized a strictly proprietary database format with virtually no capability for importing or exporting standard CSV or relational data, forcing total vendor lock-in and isolating the stratigraphic data from wider institutional IT environments.15 The system relied heavily on the native Windows "TreeView" component for data navigation. While adequate for small demonstration datasets, this architectural choice proved disastrously slow and inefficient when loading the thousands of nodes typical of a real excavation, resulting in severe performance bottlenecks.15 The software also lacked sophisticated querying tools, effectively rendering it an expensive data-entry silo.15  
The Harris Matrix Composer (HMC), developed by researchers at the Vienna University of Technology (TU Wien), currently stands as the most sophisticated and widely utilized proprietary tool in the sector.18 Operating on Windows architectures and recently offering experimental support for macOS (including M-series silicon), HMC excels at graphical manipulation and temporal reasoning.20 It formally introduced the visual distinction between positive and negative units and implemented a custom Sugiyama layout algorithm that allows users to explicitly assign absolute time intervals to matrix phases.7 Despite its technical superiority, HMC remains closed-source, commercial software.2 It operates on proprietary .hmc and .hmcx file formats, which significantly complicates interoperability with modern, web-based open-source data pipelines.20 Commercial and academic archaeologists frequently cite its licensing costs and closed ecosystem as primary barriers to seamless post-excavation efficiency, sparking demand for an open-source alternative with equivalent capabilities.21  
To bridge the gap between legacy tools and modern demands, transitional and open-source systems have emerged. Stratify, developed by Irmela Herzog, is a Windows-based application that offers exceptional interoperability.22 It natively imports and exports CSV, DBase, Paradox, and the BASP extended ASCII .LST format, while exporting graphics to DXF, HPGL, MapInfo (MIF), SVG, and JPG formats.6 Stratify is functionally rich, incorporating coordinate projections derived from GIS data and executing automatic consistency checks to highlight contradictory relationships.22 However, its user interface reflects an older era of desktop development, and its source code is not openly maintained on collaborative repositories like GitHub, limiting community-driven enhancement.22  
In the purely open-source domain, stratigraphr is an R package developed by Joe Roe that provides a "tidy" computational framework for reading, analyzing, and visualizing stratigraphies as directed graphs.25 It operates cross-platform within the R environment and includes critical functions like read\_lst() to rescue data from legacy BASP and ArchEd files.12 While it provides highly advanced interfaces to the OxCal Chronological Query Language (CQL) for Bayesian radiocarbon modeling, it is strictly a command-line library devoid of a graphical user interface, severely limiting its utility for field archaeologists who lack programming expertise.25  
Phaser, developed collaboratively by Historic England and the University of South Wales, represents the modern web-based approach to stratigraphic analysis.21 Built as an open-source, responsive single-page application using the Vue reactive framework, Phaser operates entirely in the browser without server-side dependencies.21 It imports and exports data via JSON and CSV formats.21 Phaser diverges from tools like HMC by focusing heavily on temporal reasoning and data validation; it cross-references initial stratigraphic relations with external dating evidence to highlight logical inconsistencies through a sophisticated validation engine, rather than focusing solely on the manual drafting of the matrix diagram.29

## **3\. Relational Database Architectures and Export Schemas in Field Systems**

A modern Harris Matrix generator cannot exist in a vacuum; it must be designed to seamlessly ingest data from the diverse primary tools utilized during the physical excavation process. Field recording systems employ vastly different database architectures, ranging from highly normalized relational tables to semantic web triples and bespoke mobile databases. A comprehensive understanding of the export formats, schemas, and ontological structures of these major systems is absolutely crucial for engineering a universal import engine.  
Intrasis, a highly sophisticated relational Geographic Information System developed by the Swedish National Historical Museums, exemplifies the heavy desktop approach.31 It operates on a robust PostgreSQL database backend tightly coupled with an ArcGIS runtime engine.32 When exporting data for external analysis, Intrasis relies on standard formats such as Excel (XLSX), CSV, and various GIS vector files.33 The internal data structure handles stratigraphy via highly normalized relational tables. The primary table cataloging stratigraphy identifies individual contexts via the explicit column name US (Unit of Stratification) or USNr, which is mathematically constrained by PRIMARY KEY and NOT NULL rules to ensure data integrity.35 Relationships between these units are not hardcoded into flat columns but are handled dynamically via the software's "Object Browser" and "Graph View," where inter-object relations are mapped to specific ontological predicates (e.g., "Relation to other Parts").35 When an Intrasis project is flattened and exported to CSV, these dynamic relations are typically output as a secondary relational edge table or as concatenated strings within parent/child columns, requiring the matrix software to reconstruct the graph logic upon import.21  
In contrast, web-based data management toolkits offer greater flexibility but introduce significant schema variability. The Archaeological Recording Kit (ARK) is a legacy PHP/MySQL web-based platform that has been utilized in various forms for decades.37 ARK supports custom CSV and XML exports.38 The architectural challenge with ARK is that it relies on bespoke item modules and specific relationship tables, frequently utilizing a table named strat\_relations.37 Because ARK allows implementations to be highly customized per excavation, column names vary wildly; one project might use the term "posthole" while another uses "stakehole" for identical archaeological entities.38 Similarly, fields denoting superposition could be named above, overlies, or later\_than. This absolute lack of a standardized semantic vocabulary poses a major programmatic challenge for building a universal import parser.38  
Heurist addresses this variability through academic knowledge management, allowing researchers to build richly structured relational databases via a web interface without writing code.39 Heurist exports data primarily as CSV, XML, and GeoJSON.40 Its underlying data structure strictly enforces defined relationship links between records to maintain referential integrity. Relationships are constructed using specific "pointer" fields that possess distinct tripartite attributes: a source record identifier, a target record identifier, and a "relationship type" or "certainty" constraint.41 Exporting a Heurist database to generate a Harris Matrix requires the external software to query these specific pointer records to reconstruct an accurate node-and-edge list.  
The vanguard of modern heritage data management is represented by ArchesDB, an open-source platform fundamentally structured around the CIDOC-CRM (Conceptual Reference Model) to ensure absolute global data interoperability.43 For archaeological excavation data, Arches specifically utilizes the CRMarchaeo extension.45 Unlike traditional relational databases that rely on arbitrary, user-defined column names like Above or Below, Arches structures all stratigraphic data as formal semantic triples (Subject-Predicate-Object).44 The core entities and properties critical for a Harris Matrix generator include the class A8 Stratigraphic Unit, which represents the node itself.46 The edges connecting these nodes are defined by specific properties: AP11 has physical relation to is used to describe mere physical contact without asserting a temporal sequence; AP12 confines is used when an interface (such as a cut) defines the boundary of a volume; and AP13 has stratigraphic relation to is the critical property defining the relative temporal sequence necessary for building the directed graph.44 Additionally, the property AP15 is or contains remains of links a stratigraphic context directly to the physical artifacts recovered from it.46 When Arches data is exported to JSON or CSV for external processing, it requires a sophisticated translation layer within the matrix generator to convert these heavy semantic triples (e.g., A8 \-\> AP13 \-\> A8) into the simplified node-edge arrays that a lightweight graph layout algorithm requires.  
Mobile-first data capture applications present the most immediate and frequently utilized data sources for matrix generators, as they operate directly at the trench edge. iDig, an iOS-specific application developed initially for the Athenian Agora excavations, features a built-in temporal matrix generator and exports data directly via CSV.50 However, its internal data structure relies heavily on one-to-many (1:M) "Includes" relationships.53 Processing raw iDig CSV exports into a normalized relational database or an external matrix tool requires specialized Python parsing scripts to untangle these nested hierarchical relationships effectively.50 FAIMS Mobile (Federated Archaeological Information Management System) operates as an open-source, cross-platform application focused heavily on offline data collection in remote environments.54 FAIMS generates highly structured datasets, exporting them as SQLite databases and flat CSV files.55 Because FAIMS forms are entirely custom-built for each specific project's workflow, the column headers detailing stratigraphy are entirely user-defined (e.g., Relation\_Above, Relation\_Below, Cuts).55 This requires the end-user to manually map these arbitrary column names to the matrix generator's internal schema upon import, highlighting the persistent friction in archaeological data pipelines.

## **4\. Algorithmic Paradigms in Automated Stratigraphic Graph Layouts**

The mathematical and computational challenge of rendering a Harris Matrix is a classic problem in graph theory: optimizing the layout of a hierarchical Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG) while preserving rigid chronological meaning and maintaining visual aesthetics. A matrix generator cannot simply place nodes on a canvas; it must computationally deduce the flow of time.  
The undisputed theoretical and practical standard for drawing Harris Matrices is the Sugiyama framework, also known as layered graph drawing, originally developed by Kozo Sugiyama in 1981\.7 The Sugiyama algorithm aligns flawlessly with stratigraphic principles because it strictly enforces a unidirectional hierarchical flow, mirroring the relentless top-to-bottom accumulation of time dictated by the Law of Superposition. The Sugiyama method decomposes the immensely complex layout process into three distinct, sequential computational phases.  
First is Layer Assignment, or ranking. Every node representing a context is mathematically evaluated and assigned to a specific horizontal layer based on its topological sort. In an archaeological context, nodes representing the modern topsoil or contexts that have no 'above' relationships are assigned to the highest layer.9 Edges representing the stratigraphic relationships are directed strictly downward. To ensure that edges only connect nodes in adjacent layers—a requirement for the subsequent mathematical steps—the algorithm inserts invisible "dummy nodes" along any edges that span across multiple temporal layers.9  
The second phase is Crossing Minimization. In computer science, crossing minimization in bipartite graphs is an NP-hard problem, meaning the computational time required to find a perfect solution grows exponentially with the number of nodes. The algorithm must therefore utilize heuristic methods to iteratively reorder the nodes within each layer to minimize the number of times edges cross one another.9 In highly complex archaeological sites characterized by hundreds of intersecting cuts, fills, and arbitrary excavation interventions, a high volume of edge crossings severely degrades the readability and analytical utility of the diagram.9  
The final phase is Coordinate Assignment. Once the relative ordering is established, precise X and Y coordinates are assigned to each node and dummy node. The goal is to straighten the edges as much as possible, balancing the layout horizontally to ensure an aesthetically pleasing and symmetrical diagram that guides the eye naturally down the chronological sequence.9 Advanced modern implementations, such as the customized Sugiyama algorithm utilized by the Harris Matrix Composer, intercept the Layer Assignment phase to inject explicit external dating information.9 Rather than relying solely on relative topology, nodes are shifted vertically into specific bands that correspond to absolute time intervals, periods, or phases. This ensures the vertical axis represents true chronological time while simultaneously maintaining the unyielding relative stratigraphic topology dictated by the physical excavation.9  
An alternative layered approach occasionally utilized in computational graphing is the Coffman-Graham algorithm.58 While similar to the Sugiyama framework in its fundamental goal of assigning nodes to hierarchical layers, Coffman-Graham is mathematically optimized to restrict the maximum width of any given layer.58 In archaeological scenarios involving massive horizontal stratigraphy—such as open-area excavations of ancient settlement maps where hundreds of contemporary contexts are exposed and recorded simultaneously—a standard Sugiyama layout might produce a diagram so wide it becomes unreadable and impossible to print.58 Coffman-Graham constrains this horizontal width, forcing the graph into a tighter, more vertical aspect ratio. However, because it heavily relies on inserting a massive volume of dummy nodes to route edges without violating its strict width constraints, it significantly increases the time complexity and computational overhead of the subsequent crossing minimization steps, potentially causing performance issues in browser-based generators.58  
Conversely, force-directed algorithms, such as the Fruchterman-Reingold layout, are frequently used in spatial network analysis, where nodes are modeled as repelling magnets and edges as physical springs pulling them together.59 However, force-directed layouts are fundamentally and categorically unsuited for generating a primary Harris Matrix. Because these algorithms optimize purely for uniform edge lengths and cluster densities, they routinely violate the strict, top-to-bottom hierarchical axis required by the Law of Superposition. While a force-directed layout may be highly useful for visualizing the geographic distribution of artifacts across a landscape, applying it to stratigraphy destroys the rigid temporal sequencing necessary for archaeological phasing.59  
Even with optimal algorithms, two specific aesthetic challenges plague automated matrix generation. The first is the resolution of the "H-Structure." This occurs when a complex, interlocking web of cross-cutting relationships creates a shape resembling the letter H. Legacy tools like BASP routinely failed to resolve combinations of H-structures mathematically, resulting in ambiguous, overlapping diagonal line crossings that rendered the specific sequence undecipherable to the interpreter.11 The second major aesthetic hurdle is the phenomenon defined by Steve Roskams as "Roskams' Jumps." In highly complex, deep urban stratigraphy, it is often mathematically impossible to route all edges in a two-dimensional plane without at least one line jumping over another.11 A robust, modern matrix generator must reliably identify these unavoidable topological jumps and render them cleanly. This is typically achieved using a visually distinct orthogonal routing strategy, such as drawing a "bridge" or a semi-circular bypass arc where the lines intersect, ensuring the user immediately understands that the crossing lines represent a visual necessity rather than a physical stratigraphic intersection.11

## **5\. Spatiotemporal Integration: GIS and the Four-Dimensional Matrix**

Edward Harris originally conceptualized the matrix as a diagram representing four distinct dimensions: the three geometric dimensions of the physical spatial deposit, inextricably combined with the fourth dimension of relative time.1 Historically, however, technical limitations forced a severe bifurcation in the post-excavation workflow. The spatial data remained trapped in two-dimensional plan drawings or isolated CAD files, while the chronological data existed purely as a topological chart on paper or in standalone software.1 Modern community requests heavily prioritize the total integration of the Harris Matrix with Geographic Information Systems (GIS), seeking to reunite the spatial and temporal records into a single analytical environment.18  
Recent methodological advances have focused heavily on the concept of "Stratigraphy from Topography." This approach utilizes Airborne Laser Scanning (ALS) and high-resolution Digital Terrain Models (DTMs) to map complex landscapes diachronically.8 By linking intersecting topographic features on a 3D surface model directly to specific nodes within a Harris Matrix, archaeologists can create spatio-temporal animations that model the precise formation and degradation of a site over millennia.8 A primary theoretical challenge in achieving this integration is overcoming the diagrammatic abstraction inherent in the matrix itself. In a standard Harris Matrix diagram, a massive defensive ditch spanning ten meters and a small posthole spanning ten centimeters are represented by identical, uniformly sized boxes, stripping them of their volumetric reality.8 True GIS integration allows the topological nodes in the software to inherit spatial volume attributes from the database, dynamically scaling the analytical focus or linking the abstract topological node directly to its geometric reality.9  
To facilitate this deep integration, open-source matrix generators must be engineered to support universal spatial data bindings. The standard export formats requested by institutional repositories, such as the Archaeology Data Service in the UK, include specific spatial schemas.61 GeoJSON has emerged as an ideal, lightweight web standard for this purpose. It allows developers to seamlessly link JSON-based stratigraphic attributes (such as phase, date, and unit type) directly to the 2D or 3D polygon geometries representing the physical extent of a context, making it highly suitable for web-based matrix generators.61 For integration with heavy desktop GIS environments like QGIS or ArcGIS, support for Shapefiles (.shp) and the modern GeoPackage format is strictly required.50 Furthermore, retaining the ability to export to DXF and MapInfo (MIF) formats ensures backward compatibility with legacy tools like Stratify.6  
The integration of stratigraphy and spatial data is actively supported by the robust QGIS open-source ecosystem. Specialized plugins, such as the Midvatten plugin, are already utilized by geologists and archaeologists to render complex stratigraphic logs and classifications against precise spatial coordinates directly within the QGIS canvas.62 The community envisions a streamlined workflow where spatial geometries captured in the field via QField or FAIMS Mobile 50 are seamlessly joined with an external Harris Matrix generator utilizing GeoJSON primary keys. This architectural linkage would allow an archaeologist to click a specific node in their matrix software and instantly highlight the corresponding 3D volumetric polygon in their adjacent GIS viewer, effectively realizing Harris's original vision of a fully four-dimensional analytical tool.18

## **6\. Community Demands and the Open-Source Digital Heritage Ecosystem**

The design of a new Harris Matrix generator must be closely aligned with the actual demands and frustrations of practicing archaeologists. An analysis of community discourse across platforms such as open-archaeo.info, Reddit (r/Archaeology), and specialized digital humanities blogs reveals a clear consensus regarding the specific feature requests and operational requirements for modern stratigraphic software.  
The primary demand from the field is absolute data interoperability and the complete rejection of vendor lock-in. Field archaeologists and post-excavation specialists express significant frustration with proprietary file formats, such as the .hmcx files used by Harris Matrix Composer or the closed databases of legacy systems like StratiGraf.15 These proprietary formats prevent researchers from easily migrating their data between different analytical tools, archiving their raw data in institutional repositories, or applying custom data science scripts to their findings. The community explicitly requests tools that utilize transparent, human-readable file formats, specifically CSV and JSON, ensuring that the raw stratigraphic data remains accessible and usable in perpetuity, regardless of the lifespan of the software that generated it.21  
A secondary, highly prevalent demand is for mobile-friendly interfaces and offline capabilities. Excavations frequently occur in remote locations with zero internet connectivity. Archaeologists require software that can operate entirely offline, ideally on tablets or ruggedized laptops directly at the trench edge, allowing them to construct and validate the matrix in real-time as physical contexts are revealed and removed.50 This real-time validation is critical for catching logical errors—such as recording a layer as being physically above a layer that has not yet been excavated—while the physical evidence is still visible in the ground.63 The reliance on cloud-only web applications, while convenient in laboratory settings, is widely viewed as a critical point of failure for active fieldwork.56  
Furthermore, users consistently request software capable of handling massive datasets without suffering from the user interface lag and crashes that plagued earlier generations of software.15 A complex urban excavation can easily generate tens of thousands of individual stratigraphic units. Archaeologists require layout algorithms and rendering engines capable of processing and displaying these massive graphs smoothly, complete with zooming, panning, and dynamic filtering tools that allow them to isolate specific phases or discrete physical areas of the site without overwhelming the system's memory. The transition toward tools built on modern, lightweight frameworks that adhere to Frictionless Data standards reflects a maturation of the discipline, prioritizing sustainable, open architectures over isolated commercial products.

## **7\. Socio-Technical Barriers to Desktop Application Distribution**

Developing a mathematically robust and visually intuitive open-source tool represents only half the challenge; distributing that software to a global, non-technical user base operating in remote, low-bandwidth, and often highly restricted IT environments presents severe socio-technical hurdles. Field archaeologists generally lack the technical sophistication or institutional permissions required to compile software directly from source code, execute complex command-line prompts, or debug intricate package dependencies.64 Consequently, the packaging and distribution strategy is a critical, foundational pillar of the project's ultimate success and adoption rate.  
For web developers seeking to transition a JavaScript-based matrix generator into a standalone, offline desktop application, the primary structural frameworks are Electron and Tauri. Electron currently powers massive, ubiquitous applications like Visual Studio Code, Slack, and Discord. It achieves cross-platform compatibility by bundling a complete Chromium runtime and a Node.js backend directly into the application executable. However, the primary drawback of Electron for archaeological distribution is the massive bundle size. An Electron installer typically ranges from 120MB to 200MB.65 In remote excavation camps relying on metered, low-bandwidth, or satellite internet connections, downloading a 200MB application update is often logistically impossible. Tauri provides a highly attractive alternative. It is a newer, Rust-based framework that discards the bundled Chromium engine, relying instead on the operating system's native webview components (e.g., WebView2 on Windows, WebKit on macOS). This architectural shift results in dramatically smaller executables, often rendering applications under 20MB in size.65 For open-source archaeological tools deployed globally in challenging environments, Tauri's lightweight footprint represents a massive logistical and operational advantage.  
However, regardless of the chosen framework, the most severe barrier to distributing open-source desktop software to archaeologists is the modern operating system security model, specifically concerning cryptographic trust and code signing.64 If a developer attempts to distribute an unsigned Windows installer (.msi or .exe) directly from a website or GitHub repository, the Windows Defender SmartScreen filter will intercept the file and throw a severe, full-screen warning claiming the application is unsafe and actively preventing execution.66 Bypassing this security screen requires the non-technical user to click through hidden "More Info" prompts—a process that instills deep distrust and effectively kills user adoption. Preventing this requires the developer to purchase an Extended Validation (EV) or Organization Validated (OV) Code Signing Certificate, a process that is financially expensive, bureaucratically complex, and often prohibitive for unfunded, volunteer-driven open-source projects.65 Apple has fortified macOS with a similarly restrictive system known as "Gatekeeper." Distributing a direct-download DMG file for Mac users requires not only purchasing an Apple Developer ID certificate but also submitting the application through an automated "Notarization" process where Apple's servers scan the binary for malicious code. Without this notarization, macOS will outright refuse to open the application, citing potential malware risks.65  
These cryptographic barriers also directly impact long-term maintenance. Because archaeological databases must frequently adapt to shifting site conditions and newly discovered data formats, software must remain updated. However, built-in auto-updaters (such as electron-updater) will silently fail to execute if the application binary is not properly and securely code-signed, leaving users stranded on outdated, buggy versions of the software in the field.64  
While Linux users comfortably rely on command-line package managers like Flatpak, AppImage, or Snap 67, Windows and Mac users absolutely require familiar, frictionless visual installers. Relying on CLI tools like Homebrew or Winget alienates the average field archaeologist entirely. The most successful open-source digital heritage tools, such as OpenDroneMap or various QGIS plugins, either absorb the recurring financial cost of code signing to provide native, trusted MSI/DMG installers, or they bypass the desktop operating system entirely.67 Deploying the matrix generator as a Progressive Web App (PWA) or a cloud-hosted web service allows the software to run within the user's existing, trusted web browser. This approach removes the installation barrier, bypasses the need for expensive code signing, and still allows for offline functionality through browser caching and local storage mechanisms, presenting a highly viable workaround for the socio-technical barriers of desktop distribution.

## **8\. Universal Data Ingestion and the Harris Matrix Data Package (HMDP)**

The lack of a standardized semantic vocabulary between disparate field recording systems remains the single largest bottleneck in the automated analysis of archaeological stratigraphy. Because every excavation project designs bespoke database schemas to suit their specific regional or chronological needs, a "posthole" in one system is functionally identical to a "stakehole" in another. More critically for graph generation, columns denoting the fundamental relationship of superposition are variously named Above, Over, Covered\_By, or Stratigraphically\_Later.38 A modern Harris Matrix generator cannot rely on hardcoded column names; it requires a universal import capability built upon a minimum viable data structure and the strict adoption of frictionless data standards.  
To generate a basic topological matrix from any external data source—whether it be a CSV, an Excel spreadsheet, or an SQL database export—a computational system requires only three fundamental data points per relationship line:

1. **Source Context Identifier:** The unique alphanumeric ID of the primary unit being recorded.  
2. **Relationship Type:** A strictly controlled vocabulary denoting the direction of the edge (e.g., Above, Below, Equals, Contemporary).  
3. **Target Context Identifier:** The unique ID of the secondary unit to which the source is stratigraphically related.

Historically, programs like BASP and ArchEd relied heavily on the .LST (List) format to encapsulate this data. The .LST format is an extended ASCII text list that proved highly idiosyncratic, difficult to maintain, and exceedingly frustrating to integrate with modern relational databases.6 The format's opacity led to the development of specialized migration scripts, such as harris2graph and the read\_lst() functions within the *stratigraphr* package, which were engineered specifically to rescue trapped data from these legacy formats and port them into modern computational graph environments.16  
To definitively solve the schema fragmentation problem, the digital archaeology community, spearheaded by researchers such as Stefano Costa and Thomas S. Dye, has conceptualized and proposed the Harris Matrix Data Package (HMDP).39 Built entirely upon the wider, established Frictionless Data specification, the HMDP standardizes the exchange of archaeological stratigraphic data into a lightweight, human-readable, and machine-actionable format that is completely agnostic to the proprietary software that originally generated the data.72  
A standard HMDP directory is elegantly simple, containing three primary files:

1. **datapackage.json**: This is the JSON metadata descriptor. It contains crucial metadata about the entire package, including the name, author, references, and most importantly, the exact structural schema of the data files. This descriptor ensures that the software can computationally validate the structure and integrity of the incoming data before attempting to parse it into a graph.70  
2. **contexts.csv**: This CSV resource table serves as the catalog of nodes. It defines the archaeological stratigraphy units, providing the unique ID, the unit-type (specifying whether it is a positive deposit or a negative cut), and associated positional or geometric metadata.72  
3. **observations.csv**: This CSV resource table serves as the catalog of edges. It records the specific stratigraphic relationships between pairs of contexts, strictly documenting the relative chronological sequence (the earlier-later relationships) required to build the vertical axis of the matrix.72

Furthermore, the specification allows for optional supplemental tables to be included within the package, accommodating more complex analytical needs. These include inferences.csv for documenting contexts that were recorded separately but are deemed to be equal, phases.csv for applying broader chronological groupings to blocks of nodes, and events.csv for aligning specific contexts with absolute radiocarbon or historical dating frameworks.73  
By adopting the HMDP schema natively as its foundational architecture, a new open-source Harris Matrix generator can entirely eliminate the historical friction associated with data import. Excavators utilizing bespoke, heavily customized tools like FAIMS Mobile, iDig, or Intrasis would simply run a lightweight local script to map their varied, arbitrary column headers into the standardized contexts.csv and observations.csv format.53 The matrix generator can then safely and reliably ingest the entire Frictionless package, utilizing the embedded JSON schema to automatically trigger the complex Sugiyama layout algorithms and seamlessly render the directed acyclic graph.70 This approach fundamentally guarantees data portability and secures the long-term archival viability of the archaeological record.
