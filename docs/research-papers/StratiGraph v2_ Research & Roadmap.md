# **Strategic Roadmapping for StratiGraph v2: Next-Generation Topological, Spatial, and Semantic Frameworks in Archaeological Excavation Infrastructure**

The transition from a static, analog stratigraphic drawing process to a dynamic, computationally rigorous digital architecture represents a fundamental paradigm shift in archaeological informatics. The current iteration of StratiGraph successfully establishes a robust minimum viable product for a browser-based, offline-capable application built upon a modern technology stack encompassing React, Cytoscape.js, and Tauri. By implementing foundational directed acyclic graph generation paired with automated transitive reduction algorithms—specifically leveraging the mathematical methodologies pioneered by Dye and Buck—the application successfully automates the logical sequencing of complex archaeological deposits. Furthermore, the integration of frictionless Harris Matrix Data Package data models, alongside deeply embedded modules such as HOARD for context-sheet artificial intelligence digitization, Trowel for automated report drafting, and Libby for Bayesian radiocarbon calibration via OxCal scripts, positions the platform at the vanguard of digital excavation tools.  
However, the subsequent phase of architectural evolution necessitates transitioning from isolated topological generation toward a fully integrated, spatiotemporal, offline-first collaborative ecosystem. The complexity of archaeological field data—characterized by immense three-dimensional spatial datasets, probabilistic temporal variables, complex semantic knowledge structures, and discontinuous network availability—demands highly specialized architectural solutions. A profound limitation of current digital workflows is the segregation of two-dimensional topological sequences (the Harris Matrix) from the three-dimensional volumetric reality of the archaeological trench, the probabilistic nature of absolute dating, and the semantic depth required to prevent language model hallucinations. This comprehensive report conducts an exhaustive investigation into the technical requirements, theoretical frameworks, and software integrations necessary to realize the StratiGraph v2 roadmap. The analysis spans advanced WebGL-based three-dimensional rendering paradigms, the embedding of Bayesian statistical visualizations within graph topology, semantic interoperability via ontology-driven knowledge graphs, and the deployment of peer-to-peer conflict-free replicated data types within an asynchronous edge-computing environment.

## **Advanced Archaeological and Spatial Integration**

The segregation of two-dimensional topological sequences from the three-dimensional volumetric reality of archaeological stratigraphy has long remained a profound limitation in digital excavation modeling. A core objective for the v2 architecture involves directly correlating graph nodes with high-resolution geometric representations of archaeological deposits, thereby enabling fluid interaction between the logic of stratigraphic succession and the spatial realities of the trench. The state-of-the-art in browser-based spatial computing offers multiple distinct trajectories for achieving this synthesis, encompassing rendering frameworks, point cloud management, programmatic volume slicing, and WebGIS integrations.

### **Web-Based Three-Dimensional Rendering Frameworks**

The deployment of massive three-dimensional datasets—often comprising hundreds of millions of triangles or points derived from dense photogrammetry or LiDAR scans—requires rendering frameworks capable of dynamic memory management and multi-resolution streaming directly within the browser context. The 3D Heritage Online Presenter (3DHOP) framework provides a highly optimized, open-source environment specifically tailored for cultural heritage applications.1 3DHOP utilizes the Nexus multi-resolution library to stream geometric data efficiently over constrained bandwidths, making it particularly viable for remote analysis scenarios or offline-first local deployments where memory overhead must be carefully managed.2 Operating on a declarative programming approach, 3DHOP enables the integration of high-resolution models into standard HTML without requiring specialized server infrastructure.1 The foundational ADS 3D Viewer project successfully demonstrated the viability of integrating 3DHOP with dynamic Data-Driven Documents (D3) representations of the Harris Matrix, allowing users to cross-reference static layers directly with an interactive topological map by binding JavaScript event listeners between the DOM elements of the matrix and the visibility parameters of the 3D scene.2  
However, 3DHOP is optimized primarily for triangulated mesh geometries rather than massive, uncompressed point clouds.6 For applications requiring direct manipulation of raw point cloud architectures, Potree represents the prevailing industry standard.8 Potree is a WebGL-based visualization engine designed to render billions of points directly in the browser by organizing point data into highly efficient octree spatial structures.10 By integrating Potree into the Tauri webview, the application can support the importation of immense raw spatial datasets without the computationally expensive intermediate necessity of mesh generation. This is particularly advantageous for rapid field deployment where daily photogrammetric point clouds are generated but not immediately meshed.  
Alternatively, the foundational Three.js library offers the maximum degree of programmatic customization.11 Utilizing Three.js directly via specific data loaders, such as the PCDLoader for Point Cloud Data formats, allows for the construction of bespoke rendering pipelines and custom shader materials, facilitating absolute control over the spatial scene down to the vertex level.12 While Three.js requires substantially more lower-level graphics programming compared to 3DHOP or Potree, it mitigates dependency on highly opinionated higher-level wrappers. This is a critical consideration for maintaining a modular, component-driven React-based frontend, where spatial state must be tightly coupled with the Cytoscape.js topological state.

| Rendering Framework | Primary Spatial Data Structure | Architectural Advantages | Implementation Trade-offs |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **3DHOP** | Multi-resolution Meshes (Nexus) | Declarative configuration, optimized specifically for cultural heritage and low bandwidths; proven integration with web-based stratigraphy.1 | Difficult to heavily customize rendering pipelines; highly opinionated DOM structure; less optimal for raw point clouds.7 |
| **Potree** | Octree Point Clouds | Unparalleled performance for billions of raw points; built-in measurement, classification, and clipping tools natively available.8 | Heavy dependency footprint; can be challenging to deeply integrate with custom React state management and unified UI systems. |
| **Three.js** | Raw Buffers / PCD | Total architectural flexibility; native integration with React (via React Three Fiber); extensive WebGL clipping capabilities.11 | Requires manual implementation of spatial indexing (e.g., octrees) and level-of-detail management for exceptionally large datasets. |

### **Topological Mapping and Programmatic Volume Slicing**

To move beyond mere side-by-side visualization, the topology of the Harris Matrix must actively command the spatial renderer. This involves mapping node interaction events within Cytoscape.js directly to visibility, opacity, and clipping parameters within the WebGL context. The implementation of continuous clustering algorithms—such as the Stratigraphically Constrained Continuous Clustering framework used for lithological unit boundaries—demonstrates how spatial covariance matrices can distinctively isolate transitional stratigraphic zones.13 By assigning distinct geometric identifiers to specific subsets of the 3D model (whether point classifications or mesh sub-objects), selecting a deposit node in the Cytoscape graph can trigger an immediate visual isolation of that exact deposit in the spatial view.  
A particularly sophisticated method for interrogating complex stratigraphic models within the browser is the programmatic application of clipping planes. Three.js natively supports material-level clipping planes, which mathematically discard rendering fragments during the rasterization phase based on mathematical half-spaces defined by normal vectors and distances from the origin.14 By dynamically instantiating an array of THREE.Plane objects derived from user input or spatial bounding boxes, the rendering engine can slice through complex volumetric arrays to expose internal stratigraphic relationships.15 Potree also natively features bounding volume clipping, which is highly effective for isolating arbitrary trenches or test pits within a wider digital elevation model or point cloud.8  
A compelling feature addition for the second-generation architecture involves programmatic, data-driven slicing. When an archaeologist selects an interface node in the Harris Matrix (representing a negative feature, a cut, or an erosional horizon), the application can automatically configure Three.js clipping planes aligned to the calculated surface normal of that specific spatial interface. This capability allows the user to visually peer through the exact depositional horizon within the browser, directly validating the topological relationships inferred by the matrix against the raw geometry of the trench.15 The ability to apply inverse clipping planes simultaneously allows the system to render everything "above" the selected matrix node in a wireframe or highly transparent material, while everything "below" is rendered opaquely, instantly communicating the law of superposition in a three-dimensional spatial context.15

### **WebGIS and Two-Dimensional Spatial Integration**

While three-dimensional integration provides profound analytical depth, highly accurate two-dimensional spatial integration remains a cornerstone of archaeological documentation. Linking the Cytoscape.js directed acyclic graph directly to a WebGIS interface (such as a Mapbox GL, Leaflet, or OpenLayers instance running within the Tauri webview) via GeoJSON payloads offers a lightweight, highly performant alternative to full 3D rendering.  
In this architecture, each node in the Harris Matrix Data Package contains a spatial payload adhering to the GeoJSON specification, defining the polygon coordinates of the stratigraphic unit within a specific Coordinate Reference System (e.g., WGS84 or a localized excavation grid). The application can deploy a local instance of a WebGIS engine, projecting daily drone orthomosaics generated from photogrammetry as base raster tiles. When a node is selected in the matrix, the application state triggers a spatial query that highlights the corresponding GeoJSON vector polygon overlaying the orthomosaic. Furthermore, utilizing spatial extensions like PostGIS (or client-side equivalents like Turf.js for offline capability), the application can programmatically verify the topological logic. For example, if the matrix asserts that Context A is physically above Context B, the spatial engine can compute the polygonal intersection of Context A and Context B. If their intersection area is zero, the system can flag a potential error in the stratigraphic logic, alerting the archaeologist to a discrepancy between the drawn plan and the logical matrix. This bidirectional validation between spatial realities and topological models elevates the application from a mere drawing tool to an active analytical engine.

## **Bayesian Geochronology and Phasing Visualization**

The integration of absolute chronological data into the relative sequence of the Harris Matrix requires visual representations that can simultaneously convey uncertainty, probability, and sequence. Traditional Harris Matrices are purely ordinal; they indicate sequence but contain no metric data regarding duration or absolute time. Bayesian chronologies, however, generated by engines such as the Oxford Radiocarbon Accelerator Unit's OxCal, produce complex probability density functions—frequently visualized as Kernel Density Estimations (KDEs)—that depict the statistical likelihood of a specific event occurring within a specific temporal band. The fundamental UI/UX challenge for StratiGraph v2 lies in integrating these highly complex, non-linear geometric shapes directly into the rigidly defined node structures of a directed acyclic graph without compromising performance.

### **Architectural Limitations and Canvas Rendering Strategies**

The primary graph visualization engine utilized in this environment, Cytoscape.js, renders exclusively via the HTML5 Canvas API to maximize rendering performance and frame rates across thousands of nodes.17 Unlike Document Object Model-based rendering libraries (such as D3.js interacting with SVG elements), Cytoscape.js does not natively support the injection of complex HTML or DOM-based chart components directly into the node structure as interactive elements.18 The rendering pipeline for Cytoscape.js prioritizes abstraction and speed; thus, customized rendering beyond basic styling typically requires either overriding the internal render loops—a highly complex and brittle approach—or superimposing a synchronized HTML layer precisely over the canvas coordinates.19 Superimposing HTML DOM elements over a canvas introduces severe performance penalties and visual desynchronization during pan and zoom gestures, particularly when handling extensive graphs with hundreds of stratigraphic units.  
A highly performant alternative involves utilizing dynamically generated data Uniform Resource Identifiers. Cytoscape.js permits the application of background images to nodes via its styling specification framework.20 Rather than requesting external static images from a server, the Libby module can programmatically compute the KDE curves derived from Bayesian probabilistic arrays locally within the client, format them into valid Scalable Vector Graphics markup, and subsequently encode them on the fly. Importantly, applying base64 encoding to vector graphics can induce performance bottlenecks and cross-browser parsing anomalies; the optimal approach requires URL-encoding the raw SVG strings, ensuring proper XML namespaces are declared (e.g., xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg"), and subsequently passing the data URI directly into the node's background-image style property.19  
The implementation sequence requires the Libby module to calculate the KDE distributions from the raw radiocarbon calibration scripts. A specialized React service subsequently constructs an SVG path string mapped to a normalized coordinate system representing the node's physical bounding box. The resulting SVG string, encompassing the probability distribution curve, the underlying area fill denoting standard deviations, and the median probability marker, is URL-encoded and injected into the Cytoscape element data array as data:image/svg+xml;utf8,....18 The node width and height properties must be dynamically bound to the bounding box dimensions of the generated SVG to prevent distortion or clipping of the probability curve.19 This elegant methodology ensures that the highly granular chronological data is visually embedded directly within the topological flow without sacrificing the hardware-accelerated canvas rendering performance.

### **Spatiotemporal Phasing and Axis Projection**

To contextualize individual probability distributions, the graph must support comprehensive chronological phasing. By manipulating Cytoscape's layout algorithms (such as the generic DAG layout or a custom Dagre implementation), nodes can be mathematically constrained to distinct horizontal or vertical bands corresponding to absolute temporal phases. The user interface can project a parallel timeline axis adjacent to the directed acyclic graph. When Bayesian modeling successfully converges within Libby, the absolute temporal coordinates constrain the vertical placement of the nodes, essentially transforming the topological Harris Matrix into a hybridized chronological flow diagram.  
By implementing continuous transition animations within Cytoscape.js, the graph can seamlessly morph from a purely relative topological layout (where vertical placement solely denotes "above/below" physical relations) to a strict spatiotemporal layout where vertical placement strictly denotes absolute time.17 This visual transformation serves as a profound analytical tool: it immediately surfaces chronological anomalies, visually identifying deposits that exhibit topological sequences (e.g., physically below another layer) that contradict their radiocarbon dating probability windows (e.g., statistically younger than the layer above). Such visual flagging allows archaeologists to rapidly identify intrusive contexts, residuality in artifact assemblages, or errors in field documentation directly within the browser interface.

## **AI and Semantic Data Integration**

The incorporation of Large Language Models into archaeological workflows—specifically through the HOARD module for context-sheet digitization and the Trowel module for automated report generation—introduces profound efficiencies. Yet, it concurrently introduces the systemic risk of hallucinated spatial and temporal relationships. Traditional methodologies involving the flattening of graph paths into linear Event-Episode-Deposit-Phase sequences strip away the multi-dimensional complexity of the site. To fully harness artificial intelligence without sacrificing empirical rigor, the architectural blueprint must migrate from flattening graph paths to maintaining a robust, queryable semantic network. The integration of formal ontological standards ensures that language models interact with a rigidly structured, logically inferred universe of facts rather than relying entirely on probabilistic text generation algorithms.

### **CIDOC-CRM and the Formalization of Stratigraphic Logic**

The Conceptual Reference Model established by the International Committee for Documentation (CIDOC-CRM) provides the foundational, internationally recognized ontology for cultural heritage data.24 Specifically, the CRMarchaeo extension is engineered to rigorously support the archaeological excavation process, capturing the nuanced spatial, temporal, and physical attributes of stratigraphic relationships.26 In this semantic framework, a distinct layer or deposit is not merely an integer ID in a JSON file, but rather a formally defined instance of the A8\_Stratigraphic\_Unit class. This class acts as a critical conceptual bridge; it is treated as semantically equivalent to both a spatial entity (geo:SpatialObject) and a temporal occurrence (time:TemporalEntity), thereby unifying physical dimension and historical time.29  
The transition from observed physical relationships in the trench to logical stratigraphic sequences—the core computational function of the Harris Matrix—is governed by the Harris Laws, such as the Law of Superposition, the Law of Original Horizontality, and the Law of Original Continuity. These laws can be rigorously formalized using the CRMinf extension, which models argumentation, scientific reasoning, and belief formulation.29 Rather than hard-coding directed acyclic graph rules into proprietary JavaScript functions, the architecture can utilize Semantic Web Rule Language representations to logically infer temporal succession from topological spatial relations. The formalization of the Law of Superposition dictates that if a spatial object covers another, a logical inference generates a temporal sequence concluding that the covering object occurred sequentially after the covered object.29

### **In-Browser Knowledge Graphs via Oxigraph**

To operate effectively in an offline-first environment, the application requires an embeddable graph database capable of executing the SPARQL Protocol and RDF Query Language. Traditional triplestores require centralized server infrastructure and continuous network connectivity, which is fundamentally incompatible with remote archaeological fieldwork. The deployment of Oxigraph resolves this critical architectural bottleneck.33 Oxigraph is a highly performant graph database implemented entirely in Rust that strictly complies with SPARQL 1.1 specifications and supports various serialization formats including Turtle, N-Triples, and JSON-LD.33  
Because the core StratiGraph environment relies on the Tauri framework, Oxigraph can be integrated natively via Rust bindings on the system backend, or compiled entirely to WebAssembly to run securely within the frontend WebView thread.35 Embedding a WebAssembly-compiled Oxigraph instance directly in the browser ensures that the entire excavation knowledge graph is locally queryable without external latency or API dependencies.35 The application state manager can continuously synchronize the visual graph layout in Cytoscape with the underlying RDF triplestore, ensuring that every visual modification immediately updates the semantic logic engine.

### **Ontology-Driven Graph Retrieval-Augmented Generation**

The integration of semantic triplestores facilitates advanced artificial intelligence applications via Retrieval-Augmented Generation processes explicitly designed for knowledge graphs (Graph RAG). Typical generative architectures struggle profoundly with tabular or flattened relational data when tasked with multi-hop reasoning (e.g., "Summarize the depositional history of the Roman floor layer and identify the artifact density of all underlying intrusive cuts"). An ontology-driven framework allows the language model to navigate the structural relations native to the archaeological domain without attempting to guess the underlying schema.25  
The implementation requires a sophisticated dual-stage text-to-SPARQL pipeline.37 In the initial phase, a human-readable schema using the Shape Expressions (ShEx) or SHACL format is automatically generated from the Oxigraph endpoint, describing all classes and properties present in the local site data.39 When the archaeologist submits a natural language query via the Trowel module, the language model is provided with a prompt containing these candidate ontology path patterns derived from the CIDOC-CRM schema.37 The model predicts the most relevant semantic paths necessary to answer the query.  
In the subsequent phase, the model generates a precise SPARQL query based on those predicted path patterns.37 The resulting query is executed locally against the WASM Oxigraph triplestore. The deterministic, highly structured results from the SPARQL execution are then fed back into the language model to synthesize a final, grounded natural language response. This architecture tightly constrains the language model to act exclusively as a natural language interface and query translator, completely neutralizing its capacity to fabricate facts, temporal sequences, or stratigraphic relationships.24 The result is an AI assistant capable of drafting highly accurate, deeply researched excavation reports by dynamically interrogating the full semantic depth of the Harris Matrix.

## **Collaboration and Field-Ready Infrastructure**

Archaeological excavation environments are characterized by extreme infrastructural hostility, typically lacking reliable power, structured local area networks, or broadband internet access. The current StratiGraph MVP paradigm of basic WebSocket synchronization is highly vulnerable to network partitioning; a single dropped connection can result in catastrophic merge conflicts when multiple field operatives digitize distinct trenches simultaneously. The foundational requisite for the second-generation application is a definitive transition to a Local-First architecture built upon robust Conflict-Free Replicated Data Types (CRDTs) and localized mesh networking protocols.

### **Evaluating CRDT Paradigms: Automerge and Yjs**

Conflict-Free Replicated Data Types provide a mathematical guarantee of eventual consistency across distributed systems without requiring a central authoritative server to arbitrate state changes. As local modifications occur on an archaeologist's tablet, they are recorded as an append-only log of granular operations; when disparate clients reconnect, these operation logs merge deterministically based on logical timestamps, entirely bypassing the necessity for manual conflict resolution interfaces.42 The architectural evaluation primarily focuses on two preeminent CRDT libraries: Automerge and Yjs.43  
Both libraries offer robust Rust implementations—Automerge natively and Yjs via its highly optimized Yrs port—making them exceptionally well-suited for deep integration within the Tauri backend environment.44 Automerge possesses profound academic roots and is highly optimized for complex, deeply nested JSON document structures.47 However, performance metrics indicate that Yjs demonstrates superior scalability regarding memory consumption and operation processing speed for massive document histories, a critical factor when dealing with long-running excavation projects generating thousands of state changes daily.42 Furthermore, the Yjs ecosystem is inherently network-agnostic and provides unparalleled modularity regarding network communication providers and database persistence adapters.45  
The implementation of Yjs within the StratiGraph architecture involves replacing the central Redux or Context state with a unified Yjs Y.Doc. This mathematical document contains shared data types—such as Y.Map for stratigraphic nodes and their properties, and Y.Array for chronological sequences and edge connections—that automatically propagate changes to listeners.42 Crucially, Yjs ensures absolute offline resilience through the y-indexeddb persistence adapter.48 All modifications executed by an archaeologist operating deep within a trench without connectivity are instantly persisted to the browser's local IndexedDB.48 When the application detects network availability, the internal vector clocks synchronize the missing operation updates with the network mesh. The synchronization process is highly efficient: Node A transmits its state vector, Node B identifies the specific operations Node A lacks, and transmits only the delta, guaranteeing that no granular data point is lost or overwritten during periods of isolation.49 The integration of the Yrs backend allows the Tauri host to act as a highly performant relay and persistence layer, potentially substituting a local SQLite database for browser-based IndexedDB storage, thereby circumventing browser storage quotas and ensuring absolute data permanence.42

### **Peer-to-Peer Mesh Networking and Ad-Hoc Discovery**

The ultimate realization of a localized collaboration environment requires the ability for field tablets to synchronize directly with one another without routing traffic through a cloud intermediary or even a central local server. In a remote trench, devices must self-organize into a functional mesh topology. This requires the integration of automated network discovery protocols and peer-to-peer transport layers.  
Multicast Domain Name System (mDNS) protocols facilitate zero-configuration service discovery over local subnets.52 By integrating Rust-based mDNS libraries—such as mdns-sd—into the Tauri backend via the tauri-plugin-network architecture, each running instance of StratiGraph can continuously broadcast its presence, current operational status, and specific cryptographic identifiers across the local wireless spectrum.53 When an archaeologist powers on their device within a trench network, the application automatically executes an mDNS discovery request, sequentially yielding the IP addresses and port configurations of all adjacent active instances without requiring any manual IP configuration.53  
Once local peers are identified, the application establishes direct communication pipelines. While traditional client-server architectures rely on TCP WebSockets connected to a central daemon, a mesh environment necessitates either ad-hoc WebSockets handled natively by the Rust backend using frameworks like Tokio, Axum, and Warp, or the implementation of WebRTC data channels directly between the browser contexts.51 Utilizing WebRTC, devices can negotiate cryptographic handshakes and exchange Yjs state vectors directly.51 The Yjs y-webrtc provider is uniquely equipped for this architecture, natively supporting peer-to-peer gossip protocols that disseminate topological updates throughout the localized cluster rapidly and efficiently.46 Furthermore, custom protocol extensions can be implemented in Rust via yrs-axum or yrs-warp to handle specific archaeological authentication payloads alongside the synchronization data.51  
To facilitate this network physically, the architecture must support operation over ad-hoc wireless networks directly device-to-device, or via portable, battery-powered wireless access points deployed at the excavation site.58 In this configuration, known as the "Edge Synchronization" model, multiple researchers can concurrently document different physical limits of the same massive stratigraphic context.57 The CRDT logic resolves the structural sequence in real-time across the ad-hoc network, instantly updating the Cytoscape layout on all connected devices as new layers are added. Upon the team's return to the excavation basehouse or a location with standard internet connectivity, a designated primary node executing the Yrs backend establishes a secure TLS connection with the centralized cloud repository and propagates the accumulated operational log, ensuring global consensus and automated backup.

## **Strategic Prioritization and Implementation Directives**

The extensive architectural enhancements delineated in this analysis represent a monumental leap in the capabilities of digital stratigraphy. However, to ensure a stable transition from the v1 MVP to a production-ready v2, these features must be approached sequentially, prioritizing elements that deliver immediate structural stability and data integrity for remote field operations over purely analytical or visual enhancements. The synthesis of these technologies produces a concrete, prioritized implementation matrix for the v2 roadmap, evaluating both technical feasibility and immediate impact on the field archaeologist.

| Phase | Feature Architecture | Core Technologies | Feasibility & Impact Analysis |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **1: Resilience** | Offline-First CRDT State Management | Tauri, Yjs, Yrs, IndexedDB (y-indexeddb) | **High Feasibility.** Migrating state to Yjs eliminates critical data loss during network dropouts and eradicates manual merge conflicts. Essential for establishing field trust.42 |
| **2: Discovery** | Ad-Hoc Mesh Synchronization | Rust mdns-sd, WebRTC (y-webrtc), Tokio/Axum | **Medium Feasibility.** Requires complex Rust/networking integration. High impact: allows multiple archaeologists to concurrently edit a single trench matrix entirely off-grid.51 |
| **3: Intelligence** | Semantic Triplestore & Ontology RAG | WebAssembly Oxigraph, CIDOC-CRM, LLM Text-to-SPARQL | **Low Feasibility.** Highly complex semantic mapping. Transformative impact: prevents AI hallucinations and converts the dataset into an interoperable, logically reasoned knowledge graph.29 |
| **4: Spatiality** | 3D Slicing & GeoJSON Mapping | Three.js (PCDLoader/Clipping Planes), WebGIS integration | **Medium Feasibility.** Demands heavy WebGL optimization. High analytical impact: permits direct visual validation of topological sequences against actual point cloud geometries and orthomosaics.12 |
| **5: Temporality** | Cytoscape KDE Ingestion | URL-encoded SVG generation | **High Feasibility.** Requires specialized math rendering but fits within existing UI paradigms. Embeds rich statistical probability curves natively within the core visual framework without performance hits.18 |

The successful deployment of this second-generation architecture will transcend the traditional limitations of digital recording. By synthesizing deterministic topological generation with rigorous semantic ontologies, highly resilient decentralized mesh networking, and high-performance, browser-based spatial rendering, the StratiGraph v2 infrastructure will provide an unprecedented capacity for real-time, evidence-based interpretation directly at the edge of the excavation. This approach ensures that the digital record is not merely a flattened reflection of the physical site, but a profound, multidimensional database capable of driving the future of computational archaeology.
